ReCollect64 Reviews No. 43

Mario's Modules 1.1.0 ~ Mar 14, 2025

Building Blocks

Mario is an ancient automaton that must explore the manufacturing dungeon and collect modules to craft moves from scratch. This ROM hack from Rovertronic placed first in the Mario Jams Element Competition, and you can see why. This could be one of the most triggering ROM hacks, or one of the most ingenious, so I have dug in deep by testing out all the moves to find out!

Using a crafting system similar to that of Scratch Coding, this game allows you to link together iconic mechanics and moves from Super Mario 64 piece by piece. In the process, you rediscover his repertoire of leaps and attacks with the aim of escaping the perilous prison.

You start out with absolutely zero moves to your name. Collecting coins to open chests, you begin to build your moveset. Each move allows a new area to become accessible. It's elegantly devised to bring you a sense of accomplishment and growth.

Puzzling Places

Though there are 24 slots for modules in your menu, there are 19 modules to collect. The main ones provide the core actions: jump, attack, cap power, and hover. There are linking pieces that are conditional, such as falling, after a second or two elapses, hitting a wall, touching the floor, or if the player taps a button. Lastly, there are extending pieces, labelled "+1", that can also be stacked to multiply the effects of the proceeding action.

Combining jumps with actions formulates moves such as jump + touch floor + jump again to automatically double jump. Adding jump + wall touch + jump again gives you an automatic wall jump. Spacing these out with a button press modifier after the "wall touch" allows you to tap the button to get the desired result. Another example, is to put jump + attack on one button, and Mario jumps and kicks at the same time. Useful for vanquishing enemies or breaking blocks that are blocking your path, just be mindful of the recoil rebounding you off the perilously thin pathways.

You will die a lot. Mario's Modules forces you to think and rethink any preconceptions you have about any of the rooms ahead of you. You ponder, "Ah, a double jump should do it"; it probably wont. You need to strategically formulate your mods and moves to progress. There is even a "gargantuan" wall with a sign that tells you to "lock in" and get better moves so that you can overcome it.

"Locking in" requires you to really consider the string of actions you want to achieve. Putting pieces together willy-nilly will get you nowhere, so tactically designing your moves is critical. There are two slots for moves, each with eight spaces for components. You cannot save your loadouts, which is time-consuming when reconfiguring your automaton self back to another speciality. The game also only employs the A and B buttons for Mario's moves.

I found it odd that adding jumps together gets you a double or triple jump, but so does adding "+1"s and then a jump, so why bother with multiple of the same piece? The answer is far more clever than I thought. Towards the end of the game, you get a "repeat" action block, which means you can combine jumps with a hover, wall jumps with this new piece, repeating the entire action-sequence from the start once more. The possibilities are cerebral and expansive!

Brilliant Brainpower

Each room in the creepy dungeon gives you one primary task to focus on. Usually this is a star or a chest that you have to make your way towards. Surrounding the main quest item can be a series of hazards such as thwomps, lava, or pits to fall down into. Most of the rooms also contain a secondary secret, or something you will have to return for later when you have the correct power-up at hand.

It's a clever thing to hide chests in places you have already explored because it serves to prove that you hadn't explored the entire area thoroughly. The only caveat is having to backtrack to each room with your new assortment of abilities so that you can utilise them in places you couldn't access before. I found that aspect monotonous and yet exciting, a weird combination of modular feelings to behold.

Luckily, the dungeon is circular. Most rooms eventually lead back to the initial hub, and gathering enough keys or moves can yield alternate exits or strategies to forge forward to the next challenge.

The game is relatively short, with just 10 stars to find, one boss to vanquish, and 19 modules in total to discover. I found the layout of the map quite confusing at first, like perhaps each door should have been colour-coded or something, but after a while you'll find your bearings and figure out what's where so that you can revisit the places you may have seen something else in the corner of your eye.

I would say that my first full playthrough took just over an hour. This included beating the last three sections of the game, which I found really fun and really tricky. Throughout, I found it strange that the Z-trigger wasn't utilised in the slightest; I missed the ability to duck and slide-kick. Perhaps this can be included in the future as a third action button.

Rovertronic has really pulled something special out of the bag with Mario's Modules. Through the torrent of available SM64 ROM hacks, this stands out for a lot of brilliant reasons. It's intelligent, mysterious, and innovative, but sadly, it's all over and done with far too swiftly. I hope this hack gets expanded upon, because it has so much potential it's unreal.

Perhaps a randomiser or additional levels can be added in the future, but one thing is for sure: the mechanics are intuitive and extremely well implemented as a basis for something larger. Hats off to Rovert for this Mario Jams winning banger!
