Xeno Crisis ~ Mar 24, 2025
Xeno Crisis

Box Office Smash

Trust me, the 80s were cool. Iconic and pioneering genre-defining movies like 1986's Aliens with Sigourney Weaver and Michael Biehn and 1987's "The Running Man", with Arnold Schwarzenegger. The latter film was cheesy as all heck, but a seriously cool concept; set in a dystopian future, people fight to survive in a televised gameshow that pits runners against larger-than-life gladiators through a labyrinthine gauntlet of dangerous backdrops.

In 1990, the video game Smash TV was created as a twin-stick shooter, set in a dystopian TV game show of the future, blasting waves of baddies and collecting cash and prizes. Sound familiar? Imagine combining that with Aliens, blasting waves of creatures to liberate the captured and enslaved while collecting dog tags and kudos for crushing the alien invasion.

Xeno Crisis draws heavily from these sources and blends gritty cooperative shoot 'em up action with multiple-choice adventure, which brought N64 fans something refreshing for 2023!

Morphing Xenos

Choosing between two marines, John Marsh, who holds the most ammo, or Sarah Ridley, who's more nimble, you begin the game by boarding the ISS Vanguard on a mission to liberate outpost 88 on Jupiter's 4th largest moon, Io.

The protagonists seem to have clear reto-inspired naming too; John Marsh is possibly a homage to the Terminator character John Connor, and Sarah Ridley is possibly an amalgamation of The Terminator's Sarah Connor and the original 1979 Alien movies director, Ridley Scott.

Bug Hunt

The gameplay is extremely compelling. Each time you play, the game uses RNG to generate a seed for the game you're playing, which means that no two games will play the same; it's really clever generative game design.

The twin-stick shooter genre is an underrated and almost lost art; it is so simple in its design but so effective in delivering palpable action. Point, shoot, rescue hotages, upgrade, move on. A formula that needs no more explanation, other than that there are boss battles that round off each series of rooms with waves of enemies.

The game features a signature rifle, shotgun, flamethrower, missile launcher and a laser gun to carve your way through the hordes. Pummelling various types of critters ranging from flying creatures to sprawling beasts, you really get gripped by the action, and Xeno Crisis becomes an engrossing fight for liberty and survival.

You have an ever-depleting ammo counter to keep an eye on and the ability to dodge-roll and even roll-cancel to get out of harm's way quicker. Should you run out of rounds, you can resort to melee with a quick knife slash for close encounters; though this should be an absolute last-ditch attempt at gaining ground because you will get swamped by enemies if left to prowl for too long.

There are various pickups strewn around the levels to collect and enhance your game. Notably, there are gas masks, stackable health/speed/weapon/armour and ammunition boosters, and dog tags to collect, which can be used to upgrade your arsenal at the end of each level.

Playing two-player cooperative, this game really shows off the goods. Many N64 games either had a single-player mode or a four-player jamboree. Very few games honed in on a high-quality cooperative aspect, but some notable titles include Perfect Dark, Jet Force Gemini, Goemon's Great Adventure, and Sin & Punishment.

The N64 could definitely have benefitted from more titles like this. The replayability is exponential thanks to randomly generated levels and the ever-changing waves of enemies.

The difficulty is quite unforgiving in this title. On easy the game is quite manageable, though you will die disgracefully "cheap" deaths against bullet-hell bosses, but the hard mode is quite something. You really have to have eyes in the back of your head and quickly spin, roll and melee yourself out of tight situations.

The Pros:

 Compelling top-down twin-stick shoot 'em up action.
 Seven environments and bosses.
 2-player cooperative mode.
 4-6 hours of randomised action.
 Stylish retro SFX and art direction.
The Cons:

 Unforgiving in places.
 Steep learning curve.
Conclusion:

Bitmap Bureau's Xeno Crisis benefits from modern ideas, effects, and influencial sci-fi pop culture references, blended with rock-solid, retro twin-stick gameplay. The N64 version shines brightly as a fantastic addition to the library, and with NTSC-U/NTSC-J and PAL cartridges available to purchase, it's a nice addition to any collection!
