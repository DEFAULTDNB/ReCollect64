Cruis'n USA

Cruis'n History

Back in 1994, Cruis'n USA hit arcades in a big way. With a sit-down cabinet amping up the immersion and 3D graphics that carved a path across America with instantly recognisable landmarks and bikini girls presenting you trophies, what was not to like?!

I must have been only 13 or 14 when I first played it. I remember finding a cabinet in France on a caravan holiday, and I will never forget the impression it left with me as well as the longing I had to see what else the "Nintendo Ultra 64" could do when it was meant to come out in "1995".

Sadly the N64's release date slipped to 1996, and even then Cruis'n USA missed the console's launch date but did eventually arrive 3 months later.

A Ripping Racer

Cruis'n USA is an arcade racing title that pioneered larger-than-life experiences, music, visuals, and style. The formula is essentially very similar to the pseudo-3D two-dimensional racers that came before it, like Out Run or Chase H.Q., but this time it was in stunning 3D that pushed the envelope and forged a path that raised the bar for racers that proceeded it.

Tearing across 10 levels and 4 hidden levels, you blast around American landmarks such as Route 101, Redwood Forest, Beverly Hills, LA Freeway, Death Valley, Las Vegas, Grand Canyon, Iowa, Chicago, Indiana, Appalachia, and Washington, D.C. Each one comes with its own environmental hazards, traffic, landmarks, and heavily compressed voice clips that cement the locations in your brains.

The meat and potatos of the game relies on you beating the pack and going checkpoint to checkpoint across these locales, besting the times of anyone else who has played the game too. I really liked the menus in this game, because when you enter your arcade initials, you see a license plate get pressed, then lifted and bolted into place on the leaderboards. It's a wonderful example of how they exaggerated every little detail.

It should be noted that there are 3 additional vechicles to unlock and 4 additional stages to discover, which is exactly on par with the hidden features of the arcade edition. The console edition also holds a two-player split-screen mode where you can go head-to-head competing for times and glory, though sadly the N64 port didn't get to enjoy a 4-player mode regardless of the hardware being there for it.

Arcade Antics

This game is a sign of the times, a love letter to the world it grew up in, and a time capsule that contains a little bit of everything that made the mid to late 90s such a wild time to be alive. As an early 3D title, this game has no real physics engine or automotive technical know-how going on under the hood; instead it opts for exaggerated flips and spins when you crash or trade paint.

The music is outrageously earworm-like too, with just 7 tracks that loop over and over (thankfully you can shuffle them to the ones you enjoy the most), the announcer/singer is over the top, and the accompanying sound effects and voice clips are extremely remedial by today's standards.

Playing the game, you have to keep your wits about you and your reaction speed sharp, because as the levels progress, they become increasingly twisty and contain greater numbers of obstacles to avoid. The strategy is to stick to the tarmac, don't hit anything or go off-road and pin that gas pedal to the floor throughout. It can be quite exhilarating nailing the routes, especially when you're not familiar with them anymore, and obliterating lap times gives you quite the endorphin boost!

Using manual gear shifting feels worthless. I honestly only use the manual mode when playing with a wheel because playing with automatic transmission just makes far more sense when it comes to thrashing the track and avoiding spills. Perhaps it adds more enjoyment for racing purists? Perhaps it does. But as an arcade-style racer, it removes the banal rigmarole of focusing on gearing as well as your increasingly frenetic surroundings.

Ultimately, while every track can look visually different, they feel extremely samey. The amount of twists and turns becomes insane, with me thinking "Hold on, I would pretty much be driving in circles here." because it was that frantic and erratic with bends going the same way over and over. There is no doubt that there is a thrill factor to be found here, but by today's standards, it's extremely basic and difficult to truly adore in the same way that I used to love the arcade edition with teenage eyes.

The console edition certaily feels a lot more sparse too. With less traffic, fewer interactions and less visual pop. The cars and graphics generally feel smaller in scale and look far less detailed than I remember. The menus are also lacking that slight polish that made them so attractive in the arcades. It's tough to pinpoint every alteration, but it's definitely been scaled back for the console hardware overall.

Serious Censorship

The Nintendo 64 iteration lost a lot in the transition from its arcade hardware roots. Along with dialled-down graphics across the board and notably choppier frame rates than its arcade sibling, the game was also censored in several areas, which makes the game have a very different feel and loses some of the powerful authentic appeal it had back in 1994.

Firstly, road kill is removed altogether. You can no longer blast through deer and cows like they simply don't matter to humans. This makes sense because Nintendo was historically a family-friendly company that didn't wish to offend anyone or raise the ratings of their games. Doing so would have restricted younger players from being able to purchase the games or their parents wanting to purchase the games for their impressionable children.

Secondly, the major bikini girls were redrawn to have more clothing on, and a hot tub scene (featuring Bill Clinton) was removed altogether. Again, this makes sense in terms of getting the game past the board of people that rate the game's suitability, but it undeniably took away some of the features most adolescent boys would have been drawm to this game for; titillating politically-charged action!

Conclusion:

Cruis'n USA was never going to set the world on fire once it left arcades, purely because between 1994 and 1996 (in the USA, 1998 in Europe), when competitors saw what Cruis'n had essentially pioneered, they upped the ante and improved upon the essential formula and groundwork that Nintendo and Williams had provided.

The bold flashiness of the arcade was dialled down for the home release, and even though the censoring does makes sense, it inevitably took yet another layer of shine off of the original game's overall experience.
