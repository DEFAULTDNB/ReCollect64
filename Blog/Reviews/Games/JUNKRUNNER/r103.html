ReCollect64 Reviews No. 103

Junk Runner 64 V3.1 ~ Jun 25, 2026

Homebrew Heroes

Junk Runner 64 by Team Ultra Rare won the 2025 N64brew Game Jam, the theme of which was "repair". The team not only triumphed by demonstrating a radical open-world system on the N64 which blew people's minds, but they also provided an immensely playable title that felt like a true N64-era game with modern mechanics and styling that highlights the way we should be think about homebrew gaming in 2026 and beyond.

James Lambert, of Portal 64 fame, spearheaded the team, and with an overarching motivation to push the limits of the hardware, Junk Runner 64 certainly impresses on paper, but how does it really play?

Apocalyptic Aura

The game begins with Jax, the lead protagonist, setting out to repair various machines in your hometown in order to save your sick brother Samson's life. You need to travel far and wide to discover parts to fix broken-down contraptions, deliver nanites and buy upgrades for your hover bike. The town you live in is quaint, with a house, a store and a garage. The world outside, however, is vast and unmapped, so it's up to you to scour the land and unlock the topography so that you can navigate it more easily. Somehow it all looks and feels truly authentic to the way 64-bit games used to feel, and I really like it.

The scale of the map itself is utterly incredible. It takes roughly a full minute and a half to travel from the centre of the map, your home, to any of the four corners, so a round trip across the entire map takes around 3 minutes of real-world time to traverse corner to corner. This is longer than it takes Link to cross Hyrule in The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time, it's THAT big. Junk runner 64 forces you to explore. Nothing is laid out for you; everything is discoverable. See a square building in the distance? You can travel to it! The technology used to facilitate this feeling of boundlessness and to convey the magnitude is incredible too, and it definitely pushes the system even further towards its breaking point.

It's worth noting that Jax, Samson and the robo-NPCs look awesome. Each are animated and have little artisic nuances that give them character and charm. Jax's garments look particularly impressive, with goggles, a backpack, gloves, shorts and boots that see him properly geared up for this adventure.

Eerie Exploration

With a variety of points of interest to check out along the way, you stumble upon races to partake in, nuts, maps and servos to collect or barter with. Collecting nuts is like coins, and you can use these to upgrade your bike at the local store. Upgrading your bike also puts you in better stead to collect more nuts thanks to enhanced nut tracking, and you can also give your bike a speed boost and even buy a teleportation mechanic, whereby tapping a D-pad direction warps you to a point on the map you've already visited. There is a feeling of progression and growth throughout. You're on a quest and you're learning and evolving each step of the way.

The sound effects in-game are serviceable but minimal. There are effects for the radar detector, the engine, boosts, and clicks and pings for various interactions, but the soundtrack is where I want to focus my thoughts. The map appears to be divided into four quarters; each quarter has its own eerie and atmospheric music track to accompany its desolate and vast environment, albeit with a twist on each quadrant. The bottom left has rivers and mountains; the bottom right, vast valleys and ancient monuments, such as the cracked dome. The top left of the map is a desert-like salt flat, and the top right is a mixture of sand dunes and rocky outcrops. The music can be best described as unique and off-beat, and while it lacks earworm-like melodies, each track is still strangely mesmerising.

Wanting to explore the outer limits of the world and climb the biggest mountains, I fell through a map a couple of times, and after a trippy glitchy effect, the game does eventually reset you. You can travel on foot, but it's slow and cumbersome. By bike, it's nippy and definitely the best way to travel. You can dart through speed-boosting turquoise-blue portals to build speed and jump off objects and cliffs to get boosts, and using the R-button, you can also drift to build up even more boosts. I like this system of travel; it's ideal for this kind of adventure, especially once your hover-hog is fully upgraded. It would have been cool to see a speedometer and perhaps even mini-games for upgrading or repairing your bike to offer yet more interactivity and a feeling of deeper personalisation and customisation.

Retro Repairs

Throughout the game you're given dot-matrix printed treasure maps to locate the parts, and once you have them all bagged, you can take them back to the machine in need of fixing, which kicks off a mini-game. Essentially, you take the bits and flip them around until they glow green and fit into the broken device. It's relatively simple and rarely takes more than a few moments to complete, but it feels like a nice punctuation point for what is essentially a series of fetch quests. Completing these then allows the next expedition to begin, usually in the opposite corner of the map you just visited.

I like the sense of finding stuff in crevaces and crannies that otherwise look desolate, but the draw distance doesn't lend itself to seeing those tiny things until you're on top of them. For example, I discovered a ramp and a jump leading to a house that expanded my incomplete map, but the house was entirely invisible on the mountain plateau until I was within about 10 or 20 meters of it. Technically, the N64 is rendering chunks of a huge world, but locally, in your vicinity, things still pop in abruptly - usually cliff edges in my case!

The world is impressively huge, but I found it to look quite sparse. There is a feeling of desolation and desperation to discover the next big thing, be it a mini puzzle room or another robo-NPC to interact with. But there are simply too few, and they are scattered quite far apart. I would have liked to be able to mark my map with a pin to follow and have the map reveal the location of the NPCs or POIs I did manage to find; instead, it doesn't, and it's up to you to remember where it was you discovered it. Thank goodness for the on-bike radar; without that, I would never have located all the bits needed to upgrade my bike, win the race events, and progress further.

The Pros:

 A massive open-world adventure.
 Impressive graphics and scale.
 Plenty to collect and lots discover.
 A worthy winner of N64brew Game Jam #6.
The Cons:

 HUD contrast needs improving.
 No voices and no NPC variety.
Conclusion:

Overall, Junk runner 64 is an amazing showcase of what the N64 is still capable of. The vast landscapes, quests and sense of exploration draw you in, but it lacks a little depth when it comes to NPCs and storyline.

The foundation is here for something incredible, and hopefully it will evolve into its final form eventually. If this is essentially a tech demo for the next game, then Spell Craft should be utterly astonishing.
