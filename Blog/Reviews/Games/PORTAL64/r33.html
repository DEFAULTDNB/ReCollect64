ReCollect64 Reviews No. 33

Portal 64 V0.15.0 (DEMO) ~ Feb 10, 2025
PORTAL 64

Still (Un)alive

When the Portal 64 demake was announced back in 2022, I was elated. Portal is one of my all-time favourite games, encompassing puzzle elements within a first-person shooter/platformer. My FPS addiction came from the N64, with GoldenEye 007 and Duke Nukem 64 starting the habbit of a lifetime. With a robust physics engine, I was eager to see how this would translate to the Nintendo 64.

I originally played Portal 64's beta at the first chance possible, compiling James Lambert's (of N64 VR fame) project and producing a Z64 ROM to test on my Everdrive. I fired it up and was blown away by just how incredible it was already looking. But there wasn't much substance to the first alphas, a few levels here, a sneak peek of features to come, and a plethora of possibilities.

Recently, I poked around the now-cancelled project; it was pulled in January 2024 over legal concerns raised by Valve about using Nintendo's SDK tools, rather than something completely legal and open-source, like LibDragon. The good news is that the game made it out of beta before any of this happened.

Portal 64: First Slice (version 0.14.0) was released in 2024. I managed to find the last available beta, version 0.15.0, and I dove in, ready to check out what progress had been made since I last checked it out two years ago. For how early the progress is, it's incredibly playable and unbelievably feature-rich. The bad news is that it's massively unfinished, but it doesn't take anything away from the playability and the completely unbelievable feeling of playing a game from 2007 on your 64-bit Nintendo console!

GLaDoS of The Opportunity

Controlling the game was awkward at first; I had to swap the c-button walking setup to the stick and put the looking/aiming controls back onto the c-buttons.

In general, the controls are easy to get to grips with. The L button is used to duck, R is for interacting, Z is used to jump and A/B will fire portals when you obtain the titular gun.

From the jump, you have GLaDoS barking at you, the music playing in the test chamber, and the iconic look and feel of source games is captured 1:1. When you're walking around with the gun, it also does the little shake animation you know and love. It's unbelievable that this isn't a direct port. Sure, the textures are super low resolution, but my goodness, it manages to look the part!

Scrambling through a few test chambers, I noticed just how smooth the framerate was; it's astoundingly slick. I also noticed how the physics do exactly what I would expect them to; it's possibly the most faithful reproduction of the physics-based title you could have wished for. The developer did a truly amazing job of making this a perfect replica.

There are a couple of minor bugs, but they're mainly triggered by trying to get ahead of the game and rush through. If you take your time, you'll have a way better experience. This goes to show that the game though incredibly well made, wasn't put through a final bug check. It stands to reason, but it's still sad to think that this might never happen.

Cake Was a Lie

Nintendo loves sending out "Cease and Desist" letters. I couldn't count the number of times a great project has popped up only to be nerfed by Nintendo's lawyers.

If Portal 64 can at very least get ported over to the open-source library, I think that this will revolutionise what people can expect from N64 homebrew. Imagine Half-Life demakes, Counter-Strike or Left 4 Dead Style multiplayer games, and even brand-new games based on Lambert's bespoke engine.

As it stands, Portal 64 is an absolute must-play for anyone with a flashcart and a lust for incredible gaming on their N64. You just need to find it yourself, because I cannot link any of the files here.

The Pros:

 An astounding recreation.
 Physics engine is spot on.
 Excellent sound & lighting.
 19 test chambers to navigate.
The Cons:

 Some bugs & glitches.
 Project status: cancelled.
Conclusion:

Another proof of concept homebrew game that sadly ended up cancelled. There is a glimmer of hope though, as James Lambert has recently been streaming tutorials about developing N64 games with LibDragon.

Until then, this demake is the epitome of what N64 homebrew should embody and I hope it gets ported and continued, because it demonstrates unparalleled quality throughout.
