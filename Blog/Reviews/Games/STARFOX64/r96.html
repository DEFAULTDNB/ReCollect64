Lylat Wars / Star Fox 64 ~ Mar 24, 2026

Out Foxing Everyone

Andross has invaded the Lylat system again, so General Pepper assembles a brand-spanking elite squad to save Corneria and deal with the megalomaniac and his minions. Say less! I'm all in!

Lylat Wars is the European name for the 1997 on-rails blast 'em up known internationally as Star Fox 64. The change was due to a similarly named German company named "StarVox", so to avoid any potential legal conflict, it was altered.

Check Your G-Diffuser System

The first thing that draws you into the world of Star Fox is that of its Hollywood-esque cinematic presentation and the copious special effects such as heat haze, aquatic ambience, reflections and ricochets. The game opens with a simple, bleak premise, and you are assigned to take the reins as the level-headed heroic leader of the new Star Fox team. Your team constantly encourage as well as trash-talk throughout the in-game coms too, it's all so simple and yet so captivating!

The galaxy awaits you as you begin in a war-torn and heavily invaded vision of Corneria; you are tasked with cleaning up the streets and tackling any and all evil forces, working all the way through to Venom and the crescendo encounter with Andross. Using your trusty Arwings, the team, formed of Fox, Falco Lombardi, Peppy Hare and Slippy Toad, unleash a barrage of laser and explosive-based attacks on the enemy incursion.

As a 7-stage rail shooter with 25 possible paths across 15 levels, and with waves of enemies coming at you, the levels appear linear, yet as you progress, you'll stumble upon sub-missions whereby you need to save a crew member from attack, split up and choose an area to patrol, or follow another into an alternative "hidden" route. Each playthrough can feel entirely different, whether you expertly destroy everything in your path or accidentally let a teammate down, it's a roller coaster!

The levels span the vast underwater oceans of Aquas, treacherous lava lakes of Solar, and massive open areas of sectors X, Y, Z, or Area 6, filled with heavily armed flotillas as well as various planets, such as Corneria, Fortuna, Macbeth, Titania, Katina, the last level Venom, each jam-packed with increasingly dangerous genetically mutated creatures and varied enemies to tackle. There are nods to Alien, Star Wars, Independence Day and other great film francises too. All of which seat you firmly within the action and let you play the lead role. It's so wonderfully varied; it's easy to adore.

The colours, the direction and the music all culminate into such a gorgously expressive package. Every level feels like it's been there forever, its monumantal and punctuated with an incredibly charismatic soundtrack. Sound effects pop and ping to indicate hits and bonuses, the environments hiss and crackle with energy, enemies groan and scream, and the entire cast vocalise points of interest, frustrations and victories throughout; it's an incredibly polished sensory and cinematic pleasure to behold.

Do a Barrel Roll!

Throughout your missions, you'll hone your skill set with manoeuvres such as the laser-deflecting barrel roll, the advantage-regaining loop-the-loop and the audacious in-your-face brake check. All of these moves paired with refined flight skills will see your scores increase as you sail around the environments. These scores are used to unlock medals and give you bragging rights with your friends.

The stages are laden with set pieces, and as such, you must choose your timing to set off charged lock-on attacks to achieve maximum combo points when enemies swarm together. Collecting silver rings gains you health back, but picking up three gold rings will unlock you a medal. Collectable power-ups increase your damage output, and extra lives allow you to take one more stab at those tricky levels. Don't forget to fly through interesting formations to find bonus items, such as extra bombs and lives, and checkpoint rings to help you retain your progress.

With each level lasting around 10 minutes and about 2 hours of core gameplay, one could argue that Star Fox 64 is a relatively short game, a game that's over and done too quickly. I would argue quite the opposite. There is so much arcade-like replayability packed in here, with the alternate routes, medals, unlockables, and additional dialogue to find through trial and error; this game has about 15-20 hours of gameplay at the very least, and that doesn't include multiplayer mode!

Bosses are lethal but manageable, and the switch from linear levels to "all-range mode" means you can tactically circle and strafe during each finale. Enemies have weak points to target and masses of missiles and beams to counter or avoid, but the more efficient you are in dismantling them, the more bonus points you'll receive. Anywhere from +3 to +10 is attainable depending on your skills. So get mastering your timing and finesse those titans to maximise your scores!

Multiplayer Mayhem

The multiplayer "VS" mode has to have an honourable mention here because it doesn't just feel like a bolt-on; it is an absolute riot. With 2 to 4 player Arwing dogfights at heart within 3 arenas (Corneria, Sector Z or Katina, if Katina is unlocked in single player) and 3 modes (Point Match, Battle Royale or Time Trial), you can also unlock Landmaster tanks and even a hidden "on-foot" mode with bazookas if you're good enough at earning those medals across all stages!

I have massively fond memories of playing Lylat Wars multiplayer with my family and friends during the late 90s, and as basic as it may appear now, it still has buckets of nostalgic value, especially with the exhilaration of winning or the utter agony of dealing with a crushing defeat.

The Pros:

 15 stages to master and a slew of bosses to thwart.
 Cinamatic and sprawling character-filled levels.
 Character chatter is brilliantly iconic.
 Music and SFX are top notch throughout.
 Multiplayer modes and challenges to beat.
 Intense single-player replayability.
The Cons:

 Perhaps a little short?
Conclusion:

Lylat Wars / Star Fox 64 is outstanding, even by today's expectations. From the opening title screen and beyond, everything feels expertly designed, and each level feels organic as paths unfold and your team bonds over your shared expeditions.

This game showcases so much of what Nintendo did best in this era, with an undeniable blockbuster of a sequel to Starwing, looking and playing far more evolved than its already impressive 16-bit SNES counterpart, thanks to the power of the 64-bit machine.
