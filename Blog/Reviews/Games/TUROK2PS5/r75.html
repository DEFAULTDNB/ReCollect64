Turok 2: Seeds of Evil [PS5] ~ Sep 2, 2025

Sequel-Saurus Rex

Turok 2: Seeds of Evil is the 1998 follow-up to the 1996 classic, which improves on the original with more detailed models, HD textures (Expansion Pak support), more weapons and more refined environments. This version, the remaster, released in 2021 is the version I'll be replaying for this review!

Joshua Fireseed is the new "Turok" in this game, with the status of "Turok" being an inherited/earned title which translates to "Son of Stone". He's a younger, fresher face for the game, but essentially it plays like a refined version of the original, with no major deviations from what made Turok: Dinosaur Hunter so accessible and fun to play.

The levels are bigger, the weapons more gruesome, and Night Dive Studio's remaster is clearly technically superior running through the KEX engine and providing online multiplayer, but is the game a worthy sequel?

Reptilian Remains

Following the events of Tal Set in Turok: Dinosaur Hunter (T:DH), the evil Primagen was accidentally stirred into action and decides to wreak havoc on the lands. The five energy totems set to protect the world against this evil are being destroyed, and the world needs saving from this dreadful onslaught.

Progressing through the Port of Adia, Slaughter by the River of Souls, the Death Marshes, Lair of the Blind Ones, Hive of the Mantids, and the Primagen's Lightship, you're armed with 25 weapons (and a flare gun), 11 more than T:DH, and are tasked to activate distress beacons, rescue children, locate warp portals, and find the energy totem within each level.

Each level is beautifully crafted, and my favourite is quite simply "Port of Adia". The monolithic stone sculptures, dilapidated buildings and decorative fountains and waterways make for an iconic explorable area that initially grounds this sequel in a war-torn reality. The following levels each vie for more sci-fi-like locales, with the Primagen's lightship, for example, demonstrating TARDIS-like liminal spaces, huge neon lit vaults, and shiny metal texturing throughout.

I feel like the levels are nicely built and divide into either open-world or CQB maze-like areas, but almost all feel overly long. I would have far preferred a nicer balance of shorter levels and to see even more interesting environments.

The overall gameplay is updated and polished too, with some new mechanics drawn from its peers of the era, such as GoldenEye 007 and Duke Nukem 64, such as additional adventure elements and collectables (as opposed to the basic A-to-B of the original), more cutscenes and far better weapon variety, even though there are a few that really stick out, such as the Cerebral Bore and the Homing Missiles. The shotgun and flamethrower also stick out for me because of the wondefully overblown power of the space-age shotty and the rather nice liquid fire animation of the flaming-hot death-drizzle dispenser.

Gunning for Glory

With just six sprawling levels and four hard-as-nails bosses, I feel a little short-changed when it comes to single-player variety. Sure, the game displays massive technical and graphical improvements and incorporates a lot of ideas that the Iguana Entertainment team couldn't fit into the first one, such as an awesome multiplayer mode, but I can't help but feel like the game lacks some more grounded and less repetitious levels after you've fully completed the Port of Adia.

Other levels like the Death Marshes were good but didn't grip me much, though the biomechanical "Hive of the Matids" and the Styracosaurus-riding blast-em-up section of "River of Souls" certainly stand out as interesting concepts that ultimately felt like drawn-out "filler" by the time you've finished them. Regardless, Seeds of Evil remains my favourite of the trilogy, and this version of it makes no exception to that rule. If anything, it further cements one of my favourite games into the annals of time with this superb remaster.

While the boss battles were quite engaging overall, my only quibble is that the very final boss proves to be such a grind that it becomes a chore, and I found the music in that particular battle overly jarring for the final crescendo of the experience.

In summary, Turok 2: Seeds of Evil is a superb sequel which far surpasses its predecessor, much like Terminator 2: Judgement Day (1991) to The Terminator (1984), in terms of storyline, action, animations, cutscenes, visuals and the new and improved enemies you get to gun down.

The question is, can the threequel live up to its lineage by replaying it in 2025? I can't wait to find out!

The Pros:

 HD textures, less fog and smooth gameplay.
 Better storytelling throughout thanks to the cutscenes.
 Excellent enemy variation and death animations.
 Online and local multiplayer modes are awesome.
 Remappable controls and gyro aiming are brilliant.
The Cons:

 The final boss is grindy.
 Joshua Fireseed looks pissed off 24/7.
 Six levels instead of the OG's eight.
Conclusion:

Yet again Night Dive has provided the best way to replay this game in all of its glory. This port remains true to its original release but improves it by using modern technology to deliver higher frame rates and higher-definition graphics as well as an online multiplayer option.

You get a good 15-20 hours of explorative action for your money across the 6 levels, and the meaty arsenal of enemy-mulching guns you get to play with throughout is truly awesome!
