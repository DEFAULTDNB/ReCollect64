Rakugakids

No Kidding Around

The Nintendo 64 was quite a blessed console when it came to the fighting genre. We had four Mortal Kombat titles, Killer Instinct Gold, Bio F.R.E.A.K.S., two Fighter(s) Destiny games, G.A.S.P./Deadly Arts, Mace: The Dark Ages, War Gods, two Clay Fighter versions and a plethora of brilliant WWF and WCW-based westling titles.

Rakugakids from Konami is a cartoony fighter with nine varied characters in a hand-drawn-looking land. The story goes that some kids found magical crayons; their graffiti came to life and wreaked havoc on "twinkle town". Luckily the Tinkles gang find the crayons, invent their own characters and get to scrapping. It's a very thin premise, and it's not going to win any awards for creativity, but it's the gameplay that look's interesting.

Utilising the six face buttons to battle, you accrue power on your magic meter with which to unleash a magical combo. Sound familiar? It's got a kind of Street Fighter Alpha vibe!

A Colourful Cast

The nine characters that were drawn by the Tinkles gang include (kid/fighter) Andy/Astronots, DDJ/Captain Cat.Kit, Nola/Marsa, Jerry/Robot C.H.O, Clione/Beartank, Roy/Cools. Roy, Val/Mamezo, Mudgas/Inuzo, and lastly George/Darkness. If you've heard of Beartank, that's because you might have played Goemon: Mononoke Sugoroku, Konami Krazy Racers, or even Castlevania: Circle of the Moon; Konami likes to recycle!

Each character has their own stage and moveset. The moves consist of four or five main special moves as well as three magic-powered super moves. Your magic meter increases as you play, and with a swift tap of the R button together with an attacking, counter or defensive move, you will pull off a specific animation for either.

Rakugakids two-dimensional fighters in a three-dimensional world does play to the N64's strengths. With a flattened aesthetic, the game runs fluidly, and the levels have a distinct look to them. There is also a palpable nod to Parappa the Rapper undulating through its DNA, which is no bad thing, to tell the truth.

Scrappy Strategy

The fighting feels rather random. It's a button basher rather than a game of tactics. It's goofy and doesn't take itself seriously, but that doesn't stop it cheesing you and making you feel inferior with any fighting skills you thought you had. As a huge Street Fighter II fan, I definitely see the parallels with this game; it feels similar but childish, akin to a "my first Street Fighter" game for kids. Sadly, I couldn't get past the feeling that this was remedial at best and strictly targeting children as the core players, rather than adults.

The two-player mode is obviously a nice addition to the story, training and practice modes, but I doubt many will play this for longer than 15 minutes, as it's quite limited and far less realistic, tactical, fun and intense than Fighters Destiny 2 or Deadly Arts on the system. The cartoony aspect draws much of the lustre, but underneath it all is a bare-bones fighting game without any uniquely defining mechanics.

Pulling off the special moves is nice, but there aren't enough variations to them. Almost every manoeuvre is a fireball-like forward, down-forward, forward, or backwards, down-backwards, backwards followed by a aptly powered kick or punch to launch the move thusly. While it means you instantly know every characters moveset, it's uninspired and withdraws much of the skill of remembering unique moves.

Rakugakids is simple to get into but never really evolves or holds your attention. It's worth a play, but far from being the best fighter on the system.

The Pros:

 Nine characters and stages.
 Vibrant, fluid gameplay.
 2D/3D mixture is nicely presented.
The Cons:

 Button-bashy at best.
 Extremely limited in its appeal.
Conclusion:

While it's not a hardcore fighting game, I can glean the appeal it might have had amongst the younger demographics back in 1998. Today, not so much!
