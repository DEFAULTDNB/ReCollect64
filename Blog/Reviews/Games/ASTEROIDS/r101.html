Asteroids Hyper 64

Astronomical Success

Asteroids hit arcades in a big way back in 1979. The game was a huge success in arcades and then followed the home console ports in the 80s, which only served to elongate the hype surrounding its popularity.

Some 20 years later, come 1999, someone had the bright idea of porting it to the 64-bit powerhouse that was the Nintendo 64. As a game that had two decades between the first version and this iteration, was it really worth the 3D remake treatment?

Space to Manouevre

Firing up the game, you have access to single player, multiplayer, options, and high scores. The options mode is quite minimal with only save/load options and music/SFX volume sliders, and there are no high-resolution or widescreen options here. I suppose at least the latter makes sense given the screenspace needed to lay out the levels.

The multiplayer modes on offer are "Knockout" and "Colour Clear", and these allow you and up to three friends to battle it out for space supremacy, and are a nice addition to the game, rounding it off as more than just a single-player title. I didn't get to play these modes for this review, so I will simply skim over them for now, and if I do get to play them with someone willing, I will update this review.

The main campaign has three increasingly challenging difficulties, and it allows you to choose from three ships initially, which are all named after types of bladed weapons. You have Dagger, Rapier and Longsword. Additionally, after besting the game on Expert difficulty, you can unlock the superior Excalibur too. Each ship has different stats, such as rotation, speed, shield and fire power. Dagger, which is a great all-rounder, has stats that are identical to Excalibur's, except Excalibur has enhanced firepower. It's a bit of a waste that you only finally unlock a new (better) ship once you've completed the game on the hardest difficulty. I was hoping there might be one unlocked per difficulty.

Arcade Action

Technically, the game plays well with no notable slowdowns or issues. You have the expected Asteroids mechanics transplanted onto the N64's unique controller. You can turn with the analogue stick, thrust with B, and blast with A. Your ship skates around and warps from one edge of the screen back to the diametrically opposite side, traversing the entirety of the screen. In addition to this, you have a flip ability on the bottom C button which immediately turns you 180 degrees and a "Hyperspace" warp on R which inexplicably and randomly warps you around the screen sporadically to avoid danger, I guess.

The titular asteroids come in many types, shapes and sizes, with notable variants including the crystal asteroid that regrows if left too long and the ancient energy asteroids that absorb your bullets and fire them straight back at you. Upgrades feel decent, but across 60 levels it feels like there are too few, with just mines and homing missiles to write home about, and shield rechargers and 1-ups beyond that. The AI is also pretty basic, with no real challenge offered other than a bullet-hell-riddled screen and boss enemies bullishly charging towards you.

I would have liked to see something more impressive than the same old fixed-screen gameplay we had 20 years ago, but with a fresh coat of paint. The creativity that has gone into this feels extremely minimal, but at least there are some unlockables, additional multiplayer modes, and 60 levels to beat, even if it is boring as heck and destroys your thumbs tapping away for hours solidly.

Cold Meteor Shower

Asteroids Hyper 64 was never going to set the world on fire. It's neither technically impressive nor is it exciting enough to draw you in to its rather lacklustre worlds.

The campaign's story is so bland, something about clearing a path for astro-mining or some drivel, but at least the text-filled stills have music over them. The main game itself doesn't even have music playing, just a bleeping, exploding array of sounds that increasingly feel torturous to endure.

3D ships on full colour animated backgrounds sound great on paper, but in reality, every 10-15 levels the background, or zone, changes, and it's simply not enough visual pizazz or variety to stimulate the player or keep them truly hooked. With a one-hour gameplay loop too, you're lacking gripping and replayable content to really sink your teeth into. It's a faithful remake with extra content, but it's too basic by modern standards (even in 1999).

There isn't much to draw you back in other than playing through the original 1979 version or replaying "hyper 64" again using a new ship to beat your previous scores. As a physics-driven arcade game, I get it; spend a few coins on it here and there. However, as a full-price console game, this should have been way more technologically impressive or the core concept cleverly evolved into something more interesting, engaging and upwardly re-playable for the 64-bit generation.

The Pros:

 60 (not 64) levels in 6 zones with bosses.
 4 polygonal ships to choose from.
 Up to four-player multiplayer.
 1979 Asteroids is an unlockable bonus.
The Cons:

 This cost $49.99 at launch!
 Extremely repetitive.
 No in-game music.
Conclusion:

Asteroids Hyper is no where near the kind of spectacular title that you would have wanted to spend your hard-earned cash ($49.99) on in the N64's heyday. The game plays well for what it is and does offer a few more modes for multiplayer, as well as adding in boss fights and the 1979 game as an unlockable bonus, but unless you're an absolute battle-hardened Asteroids fanatic this, is really no where near worth your time or money playing this on the Nintendo 64.

I'm kind of glad that this only released in the USA. Europe and Japan definitely did not miss out on not owning this title.
