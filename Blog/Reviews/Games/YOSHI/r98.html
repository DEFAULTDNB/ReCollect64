Yoshi's Story

Yeal for Yoshi!

With 1995's insanely popular Super Mario World 2: Yoshi's Island, Nintendo had to come up with a dedicated Yoshi game to continue the clamour for more. In 1997/1998 (Japan/World) they did just that by releasing the rather cosy-feeling Yoshi's Story for the Nintendo 64, though during development it was titled "Yoshi's Island 64".

The game was re-released in 2004 on the iQue player service exclusive to China and has subsequently been released again most recently for the Nintendo Switch Online service in 2021. I haven't replayed the game, ever, so the experience should be an interesting one to get back into to recover the super happy tree and thwart baby Bowser yet again!

As a huge enjoyer of the SNES game, I recall playing this game upon release and not really appreciating it for what it was, probably because it wasn't an all-singing and all-dancing modern 3D gaming experience like Super Mario 64. I remember finding it overly simplistic; the story didn't captivate me, and it was pretty easy to beat in comparison to the previous title. Anyway, let's see how I feel about it now, almost 30 years later!

Stylised Storytime

Yoshi's Story features a gorgeous and heavily stylised 2D and 3D mixed aesthetic which almost feels like a Paper Mario title. A pop-up book makes up the options menu, with Story, Trial, Options and Practice modes available. Though I'll be focusing on the main story for this review, the practice mode allows you to get your bearings and explains mechanics, whereas the trials mode offers you a high-score challenge after each level is unlocked.

At the beginning of each wonderfully vibrant level, you find out your lucky fruit via roulette wheel-style randomiser, and you get to pick your colour of Yoshi. The fruit mechanic simply chooses a specific crop for you to lick up with the most beneficial healing properties for Yoshi's mood in that run. There are 12 of those lucky fruits per level and 30 pieces of fruit are required to be collected to fill the screen encompassing "fruit frame". Picking a Yoshi is like selecting one of your lives. You have six Yoshis and therefore six lives before you encounter a game over screen. Incidentally, the colour of the Yoshi you choose also directly alters the colour of the eggs and blocks in the game as you're playing it, which is a nice little touch.

This game is primarily a platforming title where you collect coins, fruit and in-turn health, with some additional light puzzling and plenty of angle-based shooting. There are six main biomes, with 24 levels within them. The storybook presentation is wonderfully characterful, and each world feels different enough from the others to make it a real adventure to see everything there is to see.

Some other mechanics this game includes are sniffing out secrets with the R-button, ground pounding by holding down on the stick and A when mid-air, and flutter jumping by holding the jump button. Apart from that, you can use the stick plus the B button to use Yoshi's tongue to grab or swallow things and the Z-trigger or C-buttons, plus the stick, to aim and fire eggs, which can handily ricochet off most surfaces.

Problematic Platforming

Yoshi's Story contains all the main tropes you would associate with a Nintendo-made platforming title. You have slightly tricky areas to scale, tracked platforms to ride, swimming portions, pitfalls and lava to avoid, several bosses to beat, and most importantly, secrets to discover. Most levels fly past until you encounter the end-of-chapter boss.

The bosses feel big, like massive crescendos to the levels. I really enjoyed figuring out how to beat each boss because they rely on clever individual techniques to banish them. Collecting three hearts per level ultimately unlocks new paths and new endings, so you will need to replay the game at least four times to unlock everything there is to see.

One thing I didn't enjoy is the music. It has its moments, but majoritively, the background music is repetitive to the point of annoying ear-worm inducing levels. I wish the soundtrack was more calming, and less jarring. The game is otherwise cute and charming, but I really can't tollerate that music!

Ultimately, the Nintendo 64 didn't have a massive selection of 2D platforming games, but this is another example of how 2D graphics work well on the console and look utterly superb to boot. It's just a shame that it is so basic and so easy to complete; there simply isn't enough challenge here.

The Pros:

 6 worlds, 24 levels, 2 more Yoshis & 4 endings to discover.
 Gorgeous 2D pop-up and material-based visuals throughout.
 Trials mode offers the most challenge in the game.
The Cons:

 The music becomes really annoying.
 Not enough variety with collectables.
 Rather simple story & difficulty overall.
Conclusion:

Yoshi's Story was certainly ahead of its time in terms of its wonderfully unique aesthetic, and demonstrates the core evolutionary differences between a Mario title and a Yoshi title, but the gameplay is too simple and too easy for the majority of seasoned players, especially by todays elevated expectations of a platform game.

Essentially, while it looks great, it doesn't improve anything gameplay or fun-wise over its SNES predecessor, which is disappointing, and the music, while initially cute and fun, quickly becomes intollerable.
