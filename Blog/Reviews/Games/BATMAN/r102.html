Batman Beyond

Going Batty

The Batman phenomenon was at its peak in the late 90s, with Batman Beyond following in the footsteps of the cinematic movies, but putting a unique cyberpunk neo-noir spin on the caped cruisader. Batman Beyond sees Terry McGinnis assuming the role of Batman (Bruce Wayne retired) and chasing down the bad guys. The Joker was notably voiced by Mark Hamill in both the series and the movie.

This game, Batman Beyond: Return of the Joker (or Batman of the Future: Return of the Joker in Europe), picks up the IP and attempts to turn it into a 3D "side-scrolling" beat 'em up, but how does this iteration of Batman's brooding storylines and intense action sequences translate to 64-bit gameplay?

Winging It

Starting this game, you have a little cinematic made up of comic book-esque panels that informs you that a street gang known as the Jokerz are terrorising Gotham. They have been stealing "computer operating systems" and have all but killed Bruce Wayne. You, Terry McGinnis, have to assume the cowel and battle across five levels, Gotham Air & Space, Wayne Enterprises, Arkham Asylum, Tim Drake's Lab, and the Jolly Jack Candy Factory, to save Bruce and thwart the clown prince of crime and his goons.

Each level begins with a little back and forth via Bat-Radio or whatever, and each stage is vaguely themed to look like wherever the heck it's meant to set. The problem is that first of all, it all looks the same with drab, basic graphics with very few objects in the environments to look at or interact with. Secondly, each level plays out almost identically and there is no exploration to get involved with and no wrong path to take because the levels are so short and there are so few of them.

The combat also feels extremely shallow. While technically you have a slew of moves at your disposal, you don't exactly need them. Batman can throw hands like a machine gun, and punching and kicking can zap an enemy's health bar in seconds without you taking much damage at all. I'm not sure why, but zero strategy is required to beat any of the goons or bosses either; it's like no AI was implemented at all.

Batman has access to several modes with his suit. Defensive that deflects attacks, offensive for maximum melee damage, nimble for double jumps and gliding, and standard, which is a balance of defensive and offensive with weapons and throwables. Honestly, I completed the game using none of these modes; I just hammered the punch button as fast as possible, beating the game in one sitting.

Clown Fodder

The gameplay is ridiculously linear. You read some text (no, there is no voice acting either), walk from point A to point B, beat someone up, and grab a key to proceed. There is no thought behind any of it, no tension, no action, and no intruigue. Branching rooms would have made this 10 times better, because you would have some jeopardy in taking the wrong door and getting beaten up a bit more, or needing to explore more locations to find the correct key.

Apart from jumping over obstacles, there aren't even any puzzle elements either. Picking a specific suit at the beginning feels moot, because you quickly pick up collectables that allow you to switch anyway, and all of the weapons feel badly tacked on and ultimately useless.

The worst part is by far the sound design. There is literally a looping guitar riff over the top of every level that runs for about 10 seconds and immediately starts again. There is no break from it. There are also no attempts made at incorporating voice lines into the storyboards or the game. It feels completely devoid of character and soul.

This game could best be described as a blatant cash grab, and, at worst, a complete and total waste of the DC and Warner Bros. licensing. Pretend you have chiroptophobia and avoid this game at all costs.

The Pros:

 Two difficulties.
 Several moves and gadgets to use.
 The cartridge was black, which is cool.
The Cons:

 Only 5 levels.
 No voice acting.
 Repetitive music/levels/everything.
Conclusion:

Having never played this before and never seen any gameplay or footage, I wasn't sure exactly what to expect. My expectations were plainly too high, though, because this game is utterly terrible.

The one saving grace is that its ridiculously uninspired levels and repetitive music are over and done with in around 40 minutes. If I had been a Batman Beyond fan and paid for this back in Y2K, I would have demanded a refund.
