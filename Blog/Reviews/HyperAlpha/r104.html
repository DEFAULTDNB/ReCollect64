Hyper Alpha 64 ~ Jul 15, 2026
Hyper Alpha 64

Double Trouble

Back in the mid-to-late 90s, the Nintendo 64 was making waves as the latest and greatest console with what is still widely considered to be the wildest commercial controller ever made. With just one analogue stick, you were able to run, gun and have a heck of a lot of fun in games like Super Mario 64, GoldenEye 007, Mario Kart 64, and The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time, to name but a few!

Nowadays, one analogue stick just isn't enough because we want full control over movement and camera angles. But what if we had two sticks back in the day? Well, with today's review item, we will discover that we did have access to this ahead-of-its-time feature courtesy of the Nyko Hyper Alpha 64 (HA64) controller, but it came with a few rather niche caveats.

I received this HA64 boxed, complete with Hyper Pak Plus, for £120 earlier this month as a little treat from my wife for our 10th wedding anniversary. This knocks one of my "unicorns" off of my list of cool things that are rare and usually impossible to obtain. These controllers never show up, and I had actually been watching this one on eBay since 2024!

Grasping the controller, it instantly feels like a modern pad. It even sports dual Z-triggers situated beneath the shoulder buttons, which feel great with the almost OEM-feeling handles on each side. Face button placement is decent too, with a nice ergonomic feel across them, though the L and R shoulder buttons feel a little out of reach, with only the most pad-central edge of each shoulder being depressible. I would have preferred the outer edges to be interactive as well, simply to make it easier to click them.

The D-Pad is pretty nice too. It has a clicky feel rather than spongy, and it's extremely responsive when clicking through menus or when used to strafe in games like Turok, for example.

Sticky Stuff

Released in 1999, nearing the end of the N64's life cycle, Nyko's Hyper Alpha 64 controller undoubtedly drew upon the technology of its peers. Borrowing PlayStation's DualShock (1998) sticks and the then "futuristic" stylings of SEGA's Dreamcast controller (first publicly shown in early 1999), planting these features within the confines of a $29.99 RRP Nintendo 64 controller.

The first issue with this ideology is that the N64 simply didn't have dual analogue capabilities. Instead, the 64-bit hardware used the C-buttons to click through camera angles, and therefore no games used analogue camera controls. The Nyko Hyper Alpha doesn't allow you to use the second stick as a replacement for the C-buttons (or even the D-pad), so what exactly was the purpose of this second stick?

Essentially, when using the Nintendo 64 controller, you generally hold the central and right handles. This gives your left hand access to the trigger, the start button, and the analogue stick for movement, and your right hand can hit the right shoulder button and the cluster of C-buttons as well as the A and B buttons. The Nyko HA64, absent the central prong, houses two sticks in the middle of the pad, one slightly to the left-hand side and the other slightly to the right. With a switch on the rear, you can dictate which one is engaged and which one you prefer to use. That's it.

There is no way to map the second stick to do anything other than be a second analogue stick, and there is no way to engage both sticks at once. I feel like it's a bit of an oddball, where there was so much scope for what adding a second stick could have achieved, while still maintaining a familiar button layout for almost every game.

Another issue I have with these sticks is just how twisty the thumb caps are. They swivel and pivot under your thumbs; however, this is awkward for maintaining an angle when it purposefully moves under pressure. I don't like this feature much at all, it destroys the purpose of the thumb caps being grippy and sturdy under your thumbs if they swivel.

A Stickler for The Rules

For my own sanity I wanted to test both sticks to ensure they both acted similarly. Using the Wermipls Mimi controller tester to benchmark the sticks, I noticed that while one stick acted relatively well, the second, right stick, acted bizarrely at times.

I can only assume this is because of wear and tear and the fact that this controller is now 27 years of age. The right sticks range definitely outstripped the left, which is a bad thing when it comes to sensitivity. OEM pads aim for a range of 83 - 86. Anything significantly above or below this can cause noticeable inaccuracies during gameplay.

The left stick had a dull left edge, whereas the right had an overly sensitive left edge. It's strange that both sticks have issues when pushed in the same direction, perhaps this was a manufacturing issue.

Using the sticks in-game, you can really feel the tension pushing against you. They're tough to move, but they do spring back to center snappily. Turning in Mario Kart 64 feels a little more tiring on your thumbs because of the extra strength required to push the sticks, and aiming in GoldenEye 007 and Star Fox 64 certainly feels more difficult, with precision shots requiring more effort to move the reticle and a steadier thumb to prevent the top twisting and the stick going back to center. Sweaty-thumbed players will struggle with this controller.

(Left stick 1 test / 5 tests, right stick 1 test / 5 tests)

The Pros:

 Optional stick control.
 Turbo & slow functions.
 Modern feel & dual Z-triggers.
 More robust stick springs.
 Comes bundled with a Hyper Pak Plus Memory/Rumble Pak.
The Cons:

 Cannot use both sticks at once.
 Twisty, slippery thumb caps.
 Extremely stiff stick mechanism.
 Debatable benchmarking.
Conclusion:

Nyko's Hyper Alpha 64 controller is a confusing piece of kit. On one hand it has a modern form factor which is neat, but on the other it stifles its own performance with rock-hard stick mechanisms, twisty thumb caps, and the inability to use the two sticks at the same time, or at least use the second one as an alternative to the C-buttons or D-pad depending on your preferred orientation.

The controller came bundled with a Hyper Pak Plus as standard for $29.99 back in the day, which was great value for money, but today it commands much greater prices with CIB examples, such as mine, selling for around £100-130 due to rarity. It should be noted that the 4-bank memory / 2-level intensity rumble Hyper Pak Plus also almost never comes up for sale by itself.
