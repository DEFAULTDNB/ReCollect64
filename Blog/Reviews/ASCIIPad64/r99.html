ASCII Pad 64 ~ Apr 22, 2025
ASCII Pad 64

The Reflection of Perfection

Did you know that back in the late 90s ASCII had a licensing agreement with Nintendo that allowed them to use the OEM Nintendo 64 controller's patented shape? As one of the only examples of this, ASCII effectively became a third-party manufacturer of peripherals who utilised the same buttons, sticks, materials and membranes as the official Nintendo controllers.

Not content with just reproducing the already perfect shape, from 1997 to 2000, ASCII sought to improve upon the design. The ASC-0901 added in additional functions and a brand new slew of 18 colours, including this one, the marbled red variant with a dark grey back from 1997. Did you also know that Nintendo themselves only released 6 coloured controllers at launch (32 all told)? However, there were meant to be more, including a solid purple one shown at the 1995 Nintendo Space World/Shoshinkai and Famicom Space World exhibition, under the "Ultra 64" moniker, and an unseen yet rumoured darker grey version.

Playing With Power

The biggest difference you will notice immediately is the addition of the turbo sliders. The Z, B, and A buttons have 4 modes, off, turbo, autofire, hold, and hold turbo, as well as 5 positions for automatic press speeds between (roughly) 20/30/40/50/60 taps per second, along each toggle. The L and R triggers only have 3 modes each: off, turbo and auto. There is also no slow-mo button, which is great because I find these absolutely pointless. I feel that ASCII knew this too and correctly saw fit to not include it!

In terms of feel, it's identical to the controllers you know and love. There is zero deviation from the perfection that was the OEM Nintendo 64 controller, and I love that about this pad. The turbo functions are entirely optional, so you can choose to use them or not, and for once this is a third-party controller you can hand to your gaming rivals that you know they can't moan or groan about.

Overall the buttons and D-pad are also perfectly in tune with the rest of the pad. They're tactile and easy to press and feel 1:1 as they all should. Perfection!

A Stickler for The Rules

When comparing the sticks on the OEM and the ASCII turbo, as expected, I found absolutely no severe differences or issues. In game, these pads control beautifully, and they still hold up today almost 30 years on, albeit with a little wear and tear. The ASCII Pad 64 has remarkably less wear and tear than my own OG green controller, but then perhaps it wasn't subject to years of lunch break GoldenEye 007 and Mario Kart 64 abuse as mine routinely was.

I tested GoldenEye 007 and Perfect Dark for some precision target practice, as well as a little Pilotwings 64 and Blast Corps for good measure. I noted that the stick feels smooth with a full-screen range, is responsive when turning, and it reacts perfectly with no notable nuances. The turbo buttons function well and fire off so quickly in some modes that games like International Track & Field Summer Games 2000 will aptly tell you off for cheating.

(My 27 year old OEM stick test on the left versus the ASCII Pad 64's stick test on the right)

The Pros:

 OEM look and feel.
 Original buttons, d-pad and stick.
 Phenomenal stick benchmark results.
 Additional turbo functions.
The Cons:

 18 colour variants are tough to collect!
Conclusion:

The ASCII Pad 64 is the OEM controller with just a little bit more. There is nothing not to like about it, and it at very least matches the OEM controller beat-for-beat interms of quality, comfort and reliability.
