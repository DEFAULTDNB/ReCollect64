Minish Cap N64 Port

Miniature Marvels

Back in December 2023 The Legend of Zelda: The Minish Cap decompilation was completed, but I have recently witnessed rumblings that a Nintendo 64 port is in the works based on the PC port and powered by the incredible open-source library for N64 development, Libdragon SDK.

The as yet unreleased port is in extremely early development by a single developer, 999Sian, and while it currently requires more optimisation to run at full speed on the Nintendo 64, I have already seen footage of the native code running, displaying boot screens and menus at 15 FPS, once built from the source.

I will certainly be keeping an eye out for updates on this port because it's extremely impressive to see the 32-bit 16.78 MHz ARM7TDMI ARM CPU's instructions running natively on the N64's 64-bit MIPS-based NECVR 4300 93.75 MHz CPU. For those wondering, though theoretically possible, GBA emulation on the N64 would be virtually impossible due to the architectural disparities between the systems' hardware as well as not enough "raw overhead" in terms of clockspeed and RAM on the N64 to be powerful enough to emulate GBA games at anywhere near playable speeds.

Picori Porting

The prospect of playing Game Boy Advance games natively on the Nintendo 64 is astonishing; however, it is not the first time that GBA titles have appeared on the 64-bit system. Back in 2001 when Nintendo was peddling their then latest handheld, journalists and developers had a tough time playing and testing GBA games on tiny 2.9" screens for long periods of time. The solution was an N64 cartridge called the Wide Boy 64 AGB. It was an adapter that let you plug GBA carts directly into it and run them on the Nintendo 64, a system most journalists had had in their possession since 1996.

The Wide Boy 64 AGB was never released commercially, which is a shame, but essentially the device was akin to the GameCube's Game Boy Player, a specially manufactured system add-on that let the GC play GBA carts through its high-speed EXT ports. Neither the GB Player or the Wide Boy 64 AGB relied on emulation or any trickery; they essentially had the full GBA hardware built into them but fed the video signal to a TV screen for ease of use.

While I own a GB Player, I would love to own a Wide Boy 64 AGB to be able to play GBA titles on my N64. My dream may never become reality, but now with decompilations and ports, playing GBA games is getting one step closer, and still without the need for emulation at all.
