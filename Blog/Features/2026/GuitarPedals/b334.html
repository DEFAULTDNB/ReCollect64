N64 Guitar Pedal Spotted

Pedal Power

Something extremely bizarre crossed my feed this morning by way of The Game Beaters on X/Twitter. The musicians and self-proclaimed retro console nerds posted something which can only be described as either utter musical genius or retro hardware insanity, and that thing is Thomas McDonagh's "ConsolePedal", a twin-slot guitar FX pedal that uses Nintendo 64 cartridges to produce various effects.

By combining the brilliantly named £135 "Synth Controller" Nintendo 64 cartridge with the specifically crafted £140 base pedal, you can utilise countless effects with a simple and satisfying plug-and-play action. Using either a guitar chained to the pedal and then into a guitar speaker or loudspeaker or an N64 pad as an instrumental peripheral, you can get jamming with some truly unique sounds.

 

N64 controller synthesizer. Each button is mapped to a different note between A and G with G# being possible by using the joystick as a detuner. Holding L and then pushing a button plays that note an octave down. Holding R and then pushing a button plays that note an octave up. Pressing Z cycles forward through modes. Holding L and pressing Z cycles backward through modes. All modes except 7 stop playing the note as soon as you let go of the button.

8 total modes:

-Sawtooth (5 note polyphony)

-Triple Sawtooth (Note being played + that note an octave up + that note an octave down)

-Sawtooth Triad Chords (Monophonic)

-Square (5 note polyphony)

-Triple Square (Note being played + that note an octave up + that note an octave down)

-Square Triad Chords (Monophonic)

-"Wow" Sine that keeps playing after you let go (Monophonic)

-"Wow" Sine that stops playing as soon as you let go (Monophonic)

Note:

-Requires cartridge base in order to work. Plugging into a real N64 may cause damage as the pinout is completely different.

-N64 controller not included. Official Nintendo controllers work best since some aftermarket controllers don't make a good connection with the controller port.

(Interestingly, there are two slots so carts be paired together to produce layered effects, or in the case of using the Oscilloscope, audible and visual effects. Sadly, the synthesiser is now out of stock.).

Retro Tremolo

In addition to this AIO cartridge, there have been several other cleverly-named individual carts produced that can be swapped in to change the effects, and some of these are still available on the Etsy seller's store page.

Of these you have "Grazer Phazer" (£74.53), "Buzz Fuzz" (£65.21), "Daisy Distortion" (£65.21), "Octo Fuzz" (£65.21), "High Ping Delay" (£74.53), "Toob Yella" (£65.21), and "A Silly Scope" (£74.53), which is the oscilloscope-endowed cartridge with a screen, and it's still available to purchase today.

Looking back through previous sales, it appears that there were also other cartridge models such as "Vanilla Shake Tremolo", "Bit Crusher", "The Legend of Fuzz" (with a NES-looking Zelda parody label), "Phaser", and "Delay" carts with three built-in knobs to twiddle that altered the delay effects in real time.

As a cart collector myself, I find this use of the 64-bit technology incredibly resourceful, though it's an extortionately expensive hobby when the cost of the pedal plus two cartridges will set you back upwards of £270, plus an additional and quite frankly obscene £213 postage for the pedal and £128-£137 per cartridge. I can only assume this is to do with taxes and import duties, as the seller resides in Illinois, USA.
