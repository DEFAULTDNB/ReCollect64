SharkWire is Back Online

Jawsome Y2K Products

Back in the late 90s we didn't have internet enabled consoles as we do today. In fact, the Nintendo 64 was so air-gapped without a 64DD bolted on or Morita Shogi 64 inserted, that it didn't even know what the internet was. Barring the Japanese-exclusive Randnet service for the ill-fated add-on, which only ran from December 1, 1999 to February 28, 2001, there was no online functionality for the 64-bit console.

That was until the year 2000 when InterAct (GameShark) introduced the Sharkwire to North America, a device that added a serial port and modem to your console through a custom cartridge, which contained software that allowed you to dial-up to surf the web via the Mosaic web browser, check your e-mails, view message boards, and most importantly, create, download and share game saves and cheat codes.

That service also shut down in 2003 when InterAct filed for bankruptcy. Some 23 years on, ppcasm, aka Jonathan Benjamin, has launched an unofficial yet very specialist method to getting the hardware working again, if nothing more than the nostalgic feel for the era and a demonstration of just how chaotically bad the internet actually was back then.

Something to Sink Your Teeth Into

The project already allows for SharkWire Online activation, creating/deleting users, internet over WiFi (HTTP only @ 19200bps - for now), BLE keyboards, an ESP32 AP-based configuration tool and a custom Sharkwire Online home page. The project unfortunately is not compatible with secure HTTPS sites, doesn't offer CSS/Javascript/HTML5 support, nor does it have working e-mail functionality (yet).

Bear in mind that before you can even potentially get to start using this, you will need an ESP-based clone cartridge rather than flashing the original and now extremely niche and expensive-to-obtain hardware
