N64 Turns 30!

Nintend-Oh My God, THIRTY?!

Today is June the 23rd, 2026, and it marks the 30th anniversary of our beloved Nintendo 64 console. On this day three decades ago, Nintendo revolutionised their portfolio and blew people's minds by shifting from the ageing two-dimensional 16-bit SNES console and introducing us to the fully 3D 64-bit powerhouse.

The N64 packed a 64-bit NEC VR4300 CPU running at 93.75 MHz, a 64-bit Reality Coprocessor (RCP) running at 62.5 MHz, and just 4 MB RDRAM (expandable to 8 MB with the Expansion Pak). These specs were a paradigm shift and allowed for some of the greatest 3D experiences ever witnessed on home consoles on and before Y2K. You really had to be there to appreciate just what a big deal all of this all was; it brings back such fond recollections and cements the nostalgia we share as N64 fans!

The three games that released on the same day as the NUS-001 Nintendo 64 Control Deck were Super Mario 64, Pilotwings 64, and Saikyou Habu Shogi. Super Mario 64 needs no introduction, but the game essentially catapulted Mario from the realms of 2D side-scrolling platforming directly into the world of 3D platform-adventuring with innovative features such as fluid player movement, hub worlds, and puzzle-esque collectable stars. The jump from 1990's Super Mario World on the SNES to 1996's Super Mario 64 was massive. The creation of the Nintendo 64's famous trident-shaped controller simultaneously influenced the production of SM64 too, with camera buttons and the 360-degree stick evolving within the controller to manoeuvre Mario and view the space around him. SM64 sold the most number of copies of any game on the system, capping out at just under 12 million.

Pilotwings 64 is the direct 1996 sequel to the SNES game Pilotwings which originally landed in 1990. The 64-bit game saw true three-dimensional environments to explore, serving as a technical showcase for the reality processor. Featuring four island maps and a variety of licences to attain and challenges to beat, Pilotwings 64's only real competition at the time was SM64, so most sales leaned towards the titular plumber, who was a recognised global phenomenon, rather than the flight sim.

Last but not least is the Japanese-exclusive virtual board game title Saikyou Habu Shogi by Seta. This strategic "Japanese chess" game was the first to use the controller pak for saving (Super Mario 64 and Pilotwings 64 saved to the cartridge) and was expected to be a console seller, but instead only one in one hundred console sales has a copy of Saikyou Habu Shogi attached. To this day, I have never truly played a Shogi title; I really must brush up on the rules and get this one reviewed!
