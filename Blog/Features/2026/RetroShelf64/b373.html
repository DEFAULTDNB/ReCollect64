Retro Shelf 64: TESTED!

Shelving any Doubts! ~ Jul 19, 2026

Racked-up ROMs

Following the initial release at the beginning of the month, I reached out to Thomas Satcher, the creator of Retro Shelf 64 (RS64), a brand new graphic user interface built for SummerCart64 flashcarts, to find out just how this new menu was progressing and what features we might see in the future. Thomas kindly supplied a series of pre-release builds for me to sink my teeth into and give my honest opinions about.

I tested out various iterations of the menu over the next couple of days, and I have to say that this menu is extremely straightforward to use, and exceptionally stunning to look at. With full box art, spines, cartridge art and even box backs thrown into the mix with a widescreen perspective and gorgeous internal upscaling via the A3D's 1.5.0 FW update, I absolutely love seeing the vibrant Nintendo 64 box art shown off every which way.

The presentation is unique and immaculately laid out. I love the idea of a digital game shelf; I used to actually have a physical one back in the 90s with my boxed copies of Super Mario 64, Wave Race 64, Mario Kart 64, Lylat Wars, Top Gear Rally,, and Goldeneye 007 adorning my bedroom wall. The overall look is nostalgic to the core, regardless of whether you choose to see just the spines or the entire face on boxes. You could also opt to see a row of carts if you like, but personally I preferred the big bold colour-drenched box art!

When hovering over a title, a short, looping track from the soundtrack plays. This is a really clever touch that uses WAV64 format audio files to augment this front-end's user experience. When you select the game, it animatedly flips off the shelf and expands the cover/cart art across the majority of the screen. Surrounding it are the games logo/icon and options such as muting the audio, flipping the box over to view the back, or changing the display to another mode. It's intuitive to get stuck in and checkout everything on offer, of which there are currently 58 games built in, and each one will only load if you have the relevant ROMs on your SD card too. (No ROMs are suppplied with any build of RS64.)

Upon launching the game, you're treated to a fun little loading bar-concealing CRT TV static animation, as if the console is tuning in and getting ready to play. The loading times are speedy, and every game tested worked perfectly on both my Analogue 3D FPGA unit and my OG Nintendo 64 hardware. ROMs need to be named according to the No-Intro set for best results, but I have a medley of renamed ROMs on mine, and they all seem to link to the UI and play correctly regardless of naming or where they were placed on the SD card.

Flashy Carts

The overall experience on the SummerCart64 was sublime. Everything worked as it should, and all functions were responsive and smooth. The SummerCart64 iteration is the most optimised and natural version to use. Just drag the files on, insert the cart, locate the RetroShelf64.z64, set it to auto boot on load, and you're away. You can always boot back into the original SC64 menu to load 64DD games, unsupported N64 games, ROM hacks or homebrew.

While support for the EverDrive-64 carts is being improved, I did manage to test Retro Shelf 64 on my ED-64 X7. By copying over the "US" and "Retro Shelf 64" folders, you can simply launch the RetroShelf64.z64 from the ED64 OS and load up the enhanced RS64 UI just fine. Selecting games also works great; however, the majority of games wouldn't save due to a compatible save system not yet being implemented outside of the N64Menu's codebase, and the launcher isn't configured to fall back to the "ED/OS64.V64" menu on ED64 devices. Things like these will probably be fixed in due course, but for now ED64 users can at least snapshot saves using memories on the A3D, the platform which RS64 is specifically tuned for.

During testing I noticed that some box backs were incorrect or rough around the edges, so I pointed Thomas to the ReCollect64 Collectors App box/back/cart/manual image repository, which is freely available for anyone to use, and gave full permission to pull from it whatever he needs, so we should see 100% correct artwork displayed soon. Nintendo 64DD support is not available yet, and true Japanese box art is also missing. With everything being orientated for landscape (US/EUR) box art and fitting grid-like within the UI, custom covers have been used for titles such as Sin & Punishment or Custom Robo, and the short-edge box spines were incorrectly using squished JPN box sides from the long edge too, which, though serviceable, irked me as an N64 fan. I gave all of my feedback to Thomas, who is looking into all of this, and I very much look forward to seeing his improvements over time.

The current test builds of the N64FlashcartMenu are in progress for the ED64-based flashcarts too, so we may see official support coming to this modified menu sooner rather than later. Regardless, there is plenty of scope for RS64, because it's so visually appealing and so easy to get along with. It should also be noted that while everything does look incredible, the resolution of the art used is extremely low due to what the N64 is capable of displaying, so do not expect fully readable HD box backs on any flashcart menu any time soon.

Bookshelf Buddies

I have to admit, I'm a huge fan of this GUI, and I can easily, whole heartedly recommend it to virtually anyone of any ability. I had wished to do something similar on ReCollect64's Collectors app, and while I have been toying with 3D box art for a while, I just cannot get it dialled in as well as Thomas has done with simple pseudo-3D box art within Retro Shelf 64. I may borrow inspiration from this project yet!

The biggest downsides to this software are that only 58 games are supported currently and there aren't any additional per-game settings like options for selecting exactly which version ROM to use, enabling cheats, or adding/altering artwork and games to this yourself. Given time, I'm sure Thomas will smash this out of the park by gradually supporting more titles and introducing stellar features, such as 480i output, and, of course, versions for other flashcarts such as the ED-64 and even support for devices like the Ayn Thor too.
